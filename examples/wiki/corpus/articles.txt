Marie Curie was born on 7 November 1867 in Warsaw, Poland. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, receiving it in Physics in 1903 alongside her husband Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel for their research on radioactivity.

In 1911 Marie Curie won a second Nobel Prize, this time in Chemistry, for her discovery of the elements polonium and radium. She remains the only person to have won Nobel Prizes in two different sciences.

The Great Wall of China stretches approximately 21,196 kilometers across northern China. Construction began in the 7th century BC and continued for over two millennia, with the most well-known sections built during the Ming Dynasty between 1368 and 1644.

Mount Everest, located in the Mahalangur Himal sub-range of the Himalayas, stands at 8,849 meters above sea level. Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay made the first confirmed ascent on 29 May 1953 as part of the ninth British expedition.

The Apollo 11 mission launched on 16 July 1969 from Kennedy Space Center in Florida. Commander Neil Armstrong and lunar module pilot Buzz Aldrin landed on the Moon on 20 July 1969, while Michael Collins orbited above in the command module Columbia.

Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin in September 1928 at St Mary's Hospital in London. He noticed that a mold called Penicillium notatum had contaminated a Petri dish of Staphylococcus bacteria and killed the surrounding colonies.

The Rosetta Stone was discovered in July 1799 by French soldiers near the town of Rashid (Rosetta) in the Nile Delta during Napoleon's campaign in Egypt. It contains a decree issued in Memphis in 196 BC written in three scripts: Ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs, Demotic, and Ancient Greek.

Ada Lovelace, born Augusta Ada Byron on 10 December 1815, is considered the first computer programmer. She wrote the first algorithm intended for implementation on Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine in 1843, describing how the machine could compute Bernoulli numbers.

The Mariana Trench in the western Pacific Ocean reaches a maximum depth of approximately 10,994 meters at the Challenger Deep. The first manned descent was made by Jacques Piccard and Don Walsh on 23 January 1960 aboard the bathyscaphe Trieste.

Nikola Tesla, born on 10 July 1856 in Smiljan, Croatia, developed the alternating current electrical system that became the dominant power transmission standard worldwide. His key patents on AC motors and transformers were filed between 1887 and 1891.

The Library of Alexandria, founded by Ptolemy I Soter around 283 BC, was the largest library of the ancient world. It was part of the Mouseion research institution and is estimated to have held between 40,000 and 400,000 papyrus scrolls at its peak.

The Gutenberg Bible, printed by Johannes Gutenberg in Mainz, Germany around 1455, was the first major book produced using movable metal type in Europe. Approximately 180 copies were printed, of which 49 are known to survive today.
